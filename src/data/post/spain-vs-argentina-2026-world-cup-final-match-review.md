Spain are world champions again. A tense, tactical 1-0 extra-time victory over defending champions Argentina at MetLife Stadium ended the South Americans' reign and crowned La Roja for the second time.

Substitute Ferran Torres smashed home the decisive goal in the 106th minute after Nico Williams headed Pedro Porro's deep cross back across goal. The triumph came 16 years after Spain's first World Cup crown in South Africa — and it denied Lionel Messi a fairytale back-to-back title defense in what is widely expected to be his final international match.

## First half: Tactical gridlock and physical casualties

Spain set the tempo from kick-off. Within five minutes, Dani Olmo combined with Lamine Yamal to create the game's first clear opening. Yamal cut inside from the right and fired a low shot toward the near post, drawing a sharp diving save from Emiliano Martinez.

Argentina struggled to hold the ball. Lionel Scaloni's side sat in a compact defensive low block and tried to hit Spain on the counter, but their passing lacked precision. Messi attempted to spark transitions out of midfield, but Spain's counter-press crowded him out every time.

The physical cost of Argentina's defensive work rate surfaced before half-time. Center-back Lisandro Martinez picked up an injury after a bruising duel with Mikel Oyarzabal. He was replaced by Nicolas Otamendi in the 43rd minute, leaving Scaloni to reshuffle his defense heading into the break.

Half-time arrived at 0-0. Spain had the ball, the territory, and the chances — but not the goal.

---

## Second half: Escalating tension and a crucial dismissal

Scaloni introduced Leandro Paredes for Nicolas Gonzalez at half-time, adding steel to the midfield. It did not slow Spain down. Luis de la Fuente's side continued to monopolize possession and squeeze Argentina deeper into their own half.

Argentina's backline took another hit in the 70th minute when Cristian Romero was forced off with an injury, replaced by Facundo Medina. Both starting center-backs were now out of the match. Argentina's structural cohesion began to crack.

Then came the decisive disciplinary moment. Three minutes into stoppage time, Enzo Fernandez launched a reckless challenge on 19-year-old Pau Cubarsi. Referee Vinčić showed a second yellow card. Fernandez walked. Argentina would play the entirety of extra time with ten men.

---

## Extra time: Torres delivers the defining blow

Spain camped in the Argentine half. Nico Williams thought he had broken the deadlock when he poked home a loose ball, but VAR intervened — Mikel Merino had stepped on Nicolas Otamendi's toe in the buildup. Goal disallowed.

The genuine breakthrough came just 37 seconds into the second period of extra time. Porro sent a deep, floating cross toward the back post. Williams rose to head the ball back across the face of goal. Torres arrived at the far post and smashed a first-time volley into the roof of the net.

1-0 Spain. The stadium erupted.

Argentina threw bodies forward in search of an equalizer. Messi took a short corner and delivered a dipping cross that landed on top of the net. Minutes later, Giuliano Simeone leaned back and skied a first-time effort over the crossbar. That was all Argentina could muster. The whistle blew. Spain were champions of the world.

## Tactical analysis: Possession as a defensive shield

Spain's triumph was not simply about holding the ball. It was about *where* they held it and *what* it denied Argentina.

Luis de la Fuente deployed a 4-1-2-3 that deliberately overloaded Argentina's right side. Dani Olmo drifted into the left half-space while Cucurella pushed high and wide, creating a two-on-one against Gonzalo Montiel. This forced Argentina's midfield trio — De Paul, Enzo, Mac Allister — to shift laterally to compensate. Once Argentina compressed, Spain switched play through Rodri to the opposite flank, where Yamal had isolated Tagliafico in acres of space.

Rodri was the mechanism that made the entire system function. He sat in front of the center-backs as both a passing metronome and a transition killer. Every time Argentina won the ball and tried to spring Messi or Alvarez, Rodri's positioning cut the passing lane before it opened. He completed more passes than any other player in the tournament — but the passes he *prevented* mattered more.

The xG gap tells the tactical story clearly. Spain generated 1.94 xG to Argentina's 0.22. Argentina managed just two shots across 120 minutes — neither on target. That is not a normal defensive performance. It reflects a team that never escaped Spain's territorial cage long enough to construct a meaningful attack.

Spain's possession did not just create chances. It functioned as their primary [defensive system](https://news.northeastern.edu/2026/07/19/world-cup-final-2026/), suffocating Argentina's transition game at the source.

## Turning point: Enzo Fernandez's red card

The match hinged on the 93rd minute. Enzo Fernandez's reckless challenge on Cubarsi earned a second yellow and an early exit, as [confirmed by the match officials](https://www.aljazeera.com/sports/2026/7/19/spain-battle-past-10-man-argentina-1-0-in-extra-time-to-win-2026-world-cup). Scaloni was forced to withdraw striker Julian Alvarez for defender Marcos Senesi at the start of extra time, completely removing Argentina's attacking threat.

The chain reaction was clear: Fernandez red card → Alvarez sacrificed for Senesi → Argentina's attack neutralized → relentless Spain pressure → Torres goal.

Without the dismissal, Argentina may well have held out for penalties.

## Man of the match: Ferran Torres

Ferran Torres was named the official Man of the Match. The Barcelona forward entered the pitch in the 62nd minute and provided the clinical finishing touch that had eluded his teammates for 105 minutes.

His first-time volley into the roof of the net will live forever in Spanish football history. One swing of the right boot, and the World Cup was won.

## Post-match scenes and the halftime spectacle

The trophy presentation saw US President Donald Trump and FIFA President Gianni Infantino hand the World Cup to Spain captain Rodri. Trump was met with loud boos from sections of the crowd. Tensions spilled over on the pitch too — a scuffle between Leandro Paredes, Gavi, and Eric Garcia required quick intervention from security staff.

The final also made history by staging the first-ever Super Bowl-style halftime show at a World Cup. Curated by Coldplay frontman Chris Martin, the 11-minute performance featured Madonna, Shakira, BTS, and Justin Bieber, raising over $50 million for the FIFA Global Citizen Education Fund.

The halftime show drew criticism from football purists. The production extended the traditional 15-minute break to 18 minutes for stage setup and breakdown, with critics arguing it disrupted match flow and player recovery.

- **Argentina's era ends with Messi.** At 39, Messi was unable to summon one final moment of magic. His international career ends in tears, but his legacy as the greatest is untouchable.
